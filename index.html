Paul Chan + His Letter to Young Artists During a Global Pandemic

Throughout Paul Chan's letter, I reflected on what my internal conceptions of home include, even in terms of school work and coding. On a personal level, the pandemic has made me really consider my presence and the space I take, particularly on digital platforms, as well as caused me to challenge some of habits that I had formed and if they were truly serving me. This was most readily apparent in my relationship with social media and how it at times energerized me, but most of the time, sucked energy from me. His question of "Is there a direction that doesn't point backward?" resonnated with my experience reshaping my relationship with social platforms, that rather than regressing to using them as staples for feeling a sense of connectedness (albeit many times creating a facade of connectedness, instead of true connectedness), reimagining what my relationship with it could be that doesn't include a dependency on social media as a means to feel together with others.

On a school and coding level, his article made me question my habits and routines of relying on the same conventions, visual languages and structures in my code and in my work writ large. Two quotes in particular that struck me profoundly were "Trouble is what you find and what you must endure if you wish to gain an identity" and "How we think aout what we're doing changes how we do it, and ends up reshaping us in the mix." In this class, I hope to create more trouble for myself by pushing outside my conventions of what a website or code should look like, and allowing myself to be reshaped in the process.

'Never-ending Present'

Reading Jill Lepore's article 'The Cobweb', the phrases that stood out to me were Web pages don't have to be deliverately deleted to disappear and The Web dwells in a never-ending present. It was a weird experience reading about the archiving of the web when, in my mind, it's always been something that is meant to be time-limited. That's what makes the web great - it's not a book in the sense that once it's written, it's stuck that way, but rather that it can always be updated and changed. In the context of the article, I'm glad there's archiving to catch these critical moments. But I'm also glad that, to quote again, Honestly, isn't most of the web trash? The kind of shapeshifting quality of the web allows us to make this trash, for any good reason or for no reason at all.
